Part 2: Distribution of Indigenous Populations Across Canada

In general, Indigenous populations are concentrated in the northernmost territories in Canada
(Nunavut, Yukon, and the Northwest Territories.) Despite having a small population size overall,
the Inuit peoples make up the majority of the population in Nunavut, being 85% of the population.
Indigenous people also make up 51% of the population in the Northwest Territories.
Métis people and people of the First Nations primarily live in the southern territories of Canada,
while the majority of Inuit people live in the north. Of course, Nunavut is the territory where most Inuit
people live, while Quebec and Ontario are the areas where most First Nations people live, and the majority
of Métis people live in Alberta and Ontario.

Part 3: Mental Health and Food Insecurity

1. In general, for both sexes, a smaller percent of non-indigenous people reported having poor mental health
than indigenous populations. One notable population that experiences a high percentage of poor mental health is
the First Nations population, and this trend is consistent for both sexes. Overall, a smaller percentage of males
tends to self-report having poor mental health than females.
2. A similar trend follows for percentages of the Canadian population that experiences food insecurity. The Inuit
population experiences the largest proportion of those who struggle with food insecurity, and males have a slightly
higher self-reported rate of food insecurity.
3. This relationship that the scatterplot for both sexes suggests is that non-indigenous groups struggle less with
both mental health and food insecurity since a smaller percentage self-reported experiencing these issues.
This scatterplot for self-reporting males alone shows clusters based on population group, while the relationships
for female population groups are more scattered. This suggests women have self-reported more varying rates of
poor mental health and food insecurity than men.
