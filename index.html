Overview

Our project aims to explore global tourism data and how it affects global unemployment, comparing countries with high levels of GDP with their amount of international tourist arrivals, inflation rates, unemployment rates, and other important economic attributes of interest. We gathered a dataset from Kaggle (which assembled several datasets from the World Bank focusing on international tourist arrivals per country, GDP and inflation rates per country, and tourism expenditures, among other variables). We noticed that this dataset included lots of missing data, especially in the years following the COVID-19 pandemic. We counted the number of missing data per year, and decided that the 2013-2015 range had the least amount of missing data for the most amount of countries possible. Our preprocessing involved filtering the data to only include countries that had data for all three of our focus years and getting rid of data about general geographic regions while only keeping country data. After preprocessing our data, we started to take note of outliers. Greece, for example, has the highest unemployment rate in our filtered version of the data, but their other factors (inflation, GDP, and tourism arrivals) do not seem to match the county’s high unemployment. To answer the question of why exactly Greece had such high unemployment between 2013-2015, we will do a deeper dive into its government spending and the differences between the male and female unemployment rates.
